## Page 1 - The Core Lens: Macro Is A Balance Sheet Problem

The author's dominant habit is to reject headline macro and rebuild the situation from balance sheets. A weak analysis begins with GDP, inflation, policy rates, or a news headline. A stronger analysis asks whose balance sheet is changing, whose constraint is binding, and which price is being used to force adjustment.

The first move is usually a correction of the question. Instead of asking, "Is there enough money?", ask:

- Is the money in the right form?
- Is it in the right institution?
- Is it the right maturity?
- Can it move across market segments?
- Is it usable as capital, funding, collateral, reserves, or settlement liquidity?
- What regulation blocks or taxes the movement?

This matters because a system can have abundant money and still have tight funding. A banking system can show deposit growth and still face term-funding pressure. A country can have liquidity but still face FX stress. A central bank can inject liquidity and still fail to change credit creation if capital, collateral, confidence, or regulatory incentives are binding.

AI instruction: Never summarize a macro event only by the headline variable. Convert the event into balance-sheet entries. Identify assets, liabilities, duration, currency, collateral quality, and regulatory treatment.

Template:

1. Actor: central bank, treasury, commercial bank, corporate, household, foreign investor.
2. Asset change: reserves, bonds, loans, FX, gold, property, receivables.
3. Liability change: deposits, wholesale funding, debt, equity, payable, short FX.
4. Constraint: capital, liquidity, currency mismatch, collateral, regulation, confidence.
5. Price that adjusts: interest rate, exchange rate, credit spread, gold premium, property price, equity index.

The core thesis: macro is not a story about "money" in the abstract. It is a story about balance-sheet capacity and the channels through which claims can be transformed.

## Page 2 - Start By Attacking The Assumption

Many posts begin by identifying a flawed assumption in public analysis. This is a powerful style because it does not merely disagree with the conclusion; it invalidates the bridge between data and conclusion.

Common assumption failures:

- Treating policy rates as if they directly determine deposit rates.
- Treating system liquidity as if it automatically becomes lendable credit.
- Treating central-bank tools as if they work the same way across countries.
- Treating real estate prices as if they can fall without financing consequences.
- Treating FX intervention as if it only changes reserves, not domestic liquidity and expectations.
- Treating bank profitability as if it is free income rather than capital-intensive balance-sheet expansion.
- Treating gold monetization as if the metal price is the only risk.

AI instruction: For any article, report, or market claim, extract the hidden assumption. Then classify it:

- Mechanical assumption: "A causes B directly."
- Institutional assumption: "This rule works here like it works elsewhere."
- Behavioral assumption: "Market participants will comply or react passively."
- Balance-sheet assumption: "The actor can absorb this exposure."
- Pricing assumption: "The relevant price is observed in the official market."

Then ask: What would make the assumption false?

Example structure:

"The claim says lower policy rates should lower deposit rates. That only works if policy-rate transmission is the binding channel. In Vietnam, deposit pricing can instead be driven by bank funding structure, competition for stable liabilities, credit demand, liquidity segmentation, and regulatory constraints. Therefore the policy-rate conclusion may be directionally weak even if the policy-rate data is correct."

This style is sharp because it separates data accuracy from analytical validity.

## Page 3 - Separate Quantity Of Money From Quality Of Money

A recurring theme is that "more money" does not solve every macro problem. The author often distinguishes between nominal liquidity and functional liquidity.

Functional liquidity depends on:

- Maturity: overnight money is not the same as five-year funding.
- Currency: VND liquidity cannot always solve USD shortage.
- Location: interbank liquidity is not the same as household or corporate liquidity.
- Legal form: deposits, reserves, cash, bonds, gold, and collateral are not interchangeable.
- Regulation: a balance sheet may hold liquidity that cannot be used for the desired activity.
- Confidence: participants may refuse to transform liquidity if future risk is unclear.

AI instruction: Whenever the user says "liquidity is abundant" or "liquidity is tight," force a decomposition.

Liquidity map:

- Central bank liquidity: reserves, open-market operations, refinancing.
- Interbank liquidity: TT2 rates, repo availability, collateral haircuts.
- Deposit liquidity: TT1 deposit rates, household behavior, CASA, term deposits.
- Credit liquidity: bank willingness and ability to extend loans.
- Market liquidity: depth, bid-ask spread, forced selling pressure.
- External liquidity: FX reserves, swap lines, foreign inflows, current account.

The author repeatedly shows that the contradiction "abundant money, rising rates" is not a contradiction once you distinguish these layers. A system can be liquid in reserves but short stable deposits. It can be rich in collateral but poor in usable capital. It can have monetary expansion but low velocity.

AI prompt:

"Analyze this liquidity condition by layer. Do not use the word liquidity without specifying the instrument, holder, maturity, currency, and conversion path."

## Page 4 - Central Banks Are Market Designers, Not Just Rate Setters

The posts place heavy emphasis on the State Bank of Vietnam (SBV), but the deeper idea applies to any central bank: policy is not only about setting a rate. It is about designing a market structure and incentive system.

Central-bank analysis should include:

- Operational framework: corridor, floor, ceiling, refinancing, OMO, reserve rules.
- Transmission channel: which rate should influence which market?
- Market segmentation: which actors can access which tools?
- Incentive design: who benefits from arbitrage, delay, or regulatory loopholes?
- Behavioral credibility: do participants believe the authority will defend the regime?
- Side effects: what risk migrates elsewhere?

The author often criticizes administrative controls because they can suppress the visible price while worsening the hidden pressure. Deposit caps, credit quotas, lending restrictions, or forced rate guidance can look stable while incentives deteriorate underneath.

AI instruction: Analyze a central-bank action as a mechanism, not as an announcement.

Mechanism checklist:

1. What balance sheet does the central bank change?
2. Which private balance sheets are supposed to respond?
3. What price is targeted?
4. What price is ignored?
5. What arbitrage appears?
6. What behavior is rewarded?
7. What risk is delayed rather than resolved?

Good macro analysis asks not "What did the central bank say?" but "What does this rule make rational actors do?"

## Page 5 - Policy Rates Are Not Always The Binding Price

One recurring critique is that analysts overstate the direct relationship between policy rates and market funding rates. This is especially important in systems where bank deposits, credit targets, liquidity segmentation, or administrative guidance distort transmission.

A policy rate is a signal and an operational anchor, but it is not automatically the binding price for households, corporates, banks, or foreign investors.

To analyze rate transmission, separate:

- Policy rate: official central-bank tool.
- Interbank rate: marginal cost among banks.
- Deposit rate: cost of stable liabilities from households and firms.
- Lending rate: borrower-facing credit price.
- Bond yield: market price of duration and credit risk.
- FX-implied rate: offshore/onshore currency funding pressure.
- Real rate: nominal rate adjusted for inflation or expected depreciation.

AI instruction: Before claiming that rate cuts or hikes matter, identify the channel.

Questions:

- Are banks short deposits or reserves?
- Are depositors rate-sensitive?
- Are borrowers constrained by credit availability or loan price?
- Are banks constrained by capital, liquidity ratios, or quotas?
- Is the exchange rate forcing a higher domestic interest-rate floor?
- Are official rates credible or symbolic?

The author-style conclusion often sounds like this:

"The policy-rate move is less important than the funding structure. If banks need stable TT1 deposits, and if FX pressure or credit demand keeps competition high, deposit rates may not follow the policy rate mechanically."

This is not anti-policy analysis. It is better policy analysis.

## Page 6 - FX Is A Flow Problem Before It Is A Price Problem

In the author's style, exchange-rate analysis begins with flows and constraints, not with chart patterns. The VND/USD problem is often framed as a contest between official management and private incentives.

Key FX layers:

- Trade flows: exports, imports, services, remittances.
- Capital flows: FDI, portfolio flows, repayment, carry trades.
- Official flows: reserve sales, forward sales, swap operations.
- Banking flows: deposit dollarization, corporate hedging, trade finance.
- Informal flows: black-market USD, gold channel, offshore pricing.
- Expectations: anticipated depreciation, carry compensation, credibility.

AI instruction: Do not analyze FX only by spot rate. Build a flow table.

FX flow table:

| Flow | Direction | Actor | Currency need | Timing | Price sensitivity |
|---|---:|---|---|---|---|
| Export receipts | USD inflow | Corporates | Convert or hold | Monthly | Medium |
| Import payments | USD outflow | Corporates | Buy USD | Rolling | High |
| Reserve sales | USD supply | Central bank | Defend VND | Discretionary | Policy-driven |
| Portfolio flow | In/out | Foreign investors | Allocate | Volatile | High |
| Informal demand | USD/gold | Households | Store value | Episodic | High |

Then ask:

- Is the authority solving the flow or delaying the price?
- Are forwards buying time without creating inflows?
- Is the official price diverging from the real marginal price?
- Are interest-rate differentials enough to compensate depreciation risk?

FX analysis should end with a pressure map, not a single forecast.

## Page 7 - Forwards Buy Time, They Do Not Create Flow

The author repeatedly treats forward sales and similar tools as delay mechanisms. They can smooth pressure, change timing, or reduce immediate spot demand, but they do not magically create sustainable USD inflow.

Forward intervention should be analyzed as:

- A timing transformation.
- A credibility signal.
- A contingent future obligation.
- A way to influence expectations.
- A tool that can become costly if the underlying flow does not improve.

AI instruction: When a central bank sells forwards, never stop at "supportive for FX." Ask:

1. What immediate spot pressure is avoided?
2. What future delivery obligation is created?
3. What is the implied cost relative to reserves and interest differentials?
4. Does it attract real inflows or only postpone outflows?
5. What happens if market participants expect repeated intervention?
6. Does the forward price reveal hidden stress?

Author-style framing:

"The action is not meaningless. It can reduce near-term pressure and manage expectations. But the key question is whether it changes the flow. If it only shifts demand into the future, the policy is buying time. Buying time is useful only when the time is used to repair the underlying imbalance."

This distinction is central: a tool can be operationally effective and strategically insufficient at the same time.

## Page 8 - Gold Is A Monetary Asset, Not Just A Commodity

Gold appears repeatedly in the corpus because it sits at the intersection of household savings, shadow FX demand, collateral, monetary trust, and policy design. The author's analysis treats gold as a quasi-monetary balance-sheet asset rather than simply a speculative commodity.

Gold matters because:

- It can absorb savings when trust in currency or deposits weakens.
- It can proxy for USD scarcity or depreciation expectations.
- It can become collateral-like in informal finance.
- It can create pressure on official FX management.
- It can be monetized, but monetization creates hedging and balance-sheet problems.

AI instruction: When analyzing gold monetization, separate the metal price from the funding structure.

Questions:

- Who owns the gold?
- Who borrows against it?
- Who takes price risk?
- Who takes liquidity risk?
- Is the hedge available, affordable, and enforceable?
- Does the structure create VND funding, USD demand, or both?
- What happens under a gold price spike?
- What happens under a VND depreciation?

The author's style is to challenge superficial solutions. "Short gold and hedge it" sounds simple until you map basis risk, liquidity, margining, derivative access, counterparty capacity, regulation, and the identity of the loss absorber.

Gold analysis must include the plumbing.

## Page 9 - Real Estate Is A Credit System, Not Only An Asset Class

The author often analyzes real estate through bank credit and collateral loops. Real estate is not just an investment sector. In many emerging-market banking systems, it is part of the credit creation machine.

Key loop:

1. Asset prices rise.
2. Collateral values improve.
3. Banks lend more.
4. Developers and households transact more.
5. GDP and credit growth look stronger.
6. Asset prices become harder to discipline without damaging balance sheets.

If policymakers try to force prices lower, they may also reduce collateral value, weaken borrowers, increase non-performing loans, and reduce banks' willingness to lend. That can create a macro drag even if housing affordability improves.

AI instruction: Analyze any real estate policy through the collateral-credit channel.

Checklist:

- How much bank credit is tied to real estate directly?
- How much is tied indirectly through collateral?
- Which borrowers rely on refinancing?
- Which banks are most exposed?
- Would falling prices clean the system or trigger forced deleveraging?
- Is the policy targeting price, supply, speculation, leverage, or funding?
- Who must absorb the loss?

Author-style framing:

"Reducing dependence on real estate is not simply a price-control problem. Even making prices fall requires financing, loss absorption, and a transition path for bank balance sheets."

Macro analysis should treat property as both asset and collateral infrastructure.

## Page 10 - Banks Look Profitable Because They Are Levered Factories

The author pushes against the idea that banks make easy money. Bank profits are generated by balance-sheet scale, maturity transformation, credit risk, capital usage, and regulation. High nominal profits can hide high capital intensity.

Bank analysis should include:

- NIM: spread income, but not free income.
- Credit growth: growth requires funding and capital.
- CASA: cheap deposits improve margins but are behavioral liabilities.
- Loan loss: credit risk is delayed until the cycle turns.
- Duration: asset-liability mismatch can create rate risk.
- Capital adequacy: growth consumes equity.
- Liquidity ratios: funding composition matters.
- Regulation: rules can change the economic return.

AI instruction: Do not evaluate a bank only by earnings. Build a balance-sheet constraint view.

Questions:

- Is profit driven by spread, volume, fees, trading, or provisioning release?
- What funding source supports asset growth?
- Does the bank have enough capital to grow?
- Are deposits stable or rate-sensitive?
- Are reported earnings ahead of credit losses?
- Is the loan book tied to real estate or asset monetization?
- What happens if deposit rates rise but lending rates are capped or slow to reprice?

The author's approach treats banks as macro transmission machines. If banks are constrained, policy is constrained.

## Page 11 - Regulation Changes Behavior Before It Changes Ratios

A major recurring theme is regulatory design. Regulations are not neutral constraints. They shape incentives, arbitrage, and risk migration.

Regulatory analysis should not stop at "stricter is safer." A stricter rule can make the system safer if it reduces leverage and improves resilience. But it can also push risk into less visible channels, reduce market function, or make official ratios look better while real stress grows elsewhere.

AI instruction: For every rule, ask who changes behavior and how.

Regulation map:

- Target ratio: capital, liquidity, credit growth, deposit cap, loan-to-value.
- Affected actor: bank, borrower, investor, household, broker, foreign fund.
- Intended behavior: reduce risk, slow credit, increase capital, stabilize FX.
- Unintended behavior: arbitrage, off-balance-sheet shift, informal market growth, maturity mismatch.
- Hidden cost: lower credit availability, lower profitability, distorted pricing.

Author-style question:

"Does the rule solve the economic problem or just move the problem into another balance-sheet pocket?"

AI output should always include an "incentive consequence" section. This is the part most reports miss.

## Page 12 - Basel Is A Framework, Not A Copy-Paste Solution

The author repeatedly compares Vietnam's banking regulations with international frameworks such as Basel. The implied lesson is that importing a regulatory model without importing the surrounding market infrastructure can distort outcomes.

Basel-style rules assume certain market features:

- Deep bond markets.
- Reliable credit ratings.
- Liquid collateral.
- Active repo and derivatives markets.
- Robust disclosure.
- Legal enforceability.
- Diversified investor base.
- Market-based pricing.

If those features are missing, the same rule can behave differently. It can become stricter in practice, less effective, or easier to arbitrage.

AI instruction: When comparing frameworks, identify missing institutions.

Questions:

- Does the local market have the instruments assumed by the rule?
- Can banks hedge the risk the rule measures?
- Are ratings credible and widely used?
- Are bond markets liquid enough for mark-to-market discipline?
- Do investors have alternatives?
- Does legal enforcement support collateral recovery?

Author-style conclusion:

"The imported framework is not wrong in theory. The problem is the local transmission mechanism. A portable version of a foreign model can produce a different equilibrium when market depth, incentives, and institutional capacity differ."

This is a core principle for emerging-market macro analysis.

## Page 13 - Credit Ratings Fail When The Market Does Not Need Them

The corpus includes skepticism about credit-rating development in Vietnam. The deeper framework: a financial service becomes valuable only when the market structure demands it.

Credit ratings matter when:

- Bond investors price credit risk independently.
- Regulations require ratings for capital or eligibility.
- Issuers need ratings to access funding.
- Investors can compare issuers across sectors.
- Defaults are recognized and priced.
- Legal recovery and disclosure are credible.

Ratings struggle when:

- Lending is bank-dominated.
- Relationships matter more than market pricing.
- Investors rely on implicit guarantees.
- Disclosure is weak.
- Defaults are delayed or restructured quietly.
- There is no penalty for being unrated.

AI instruction: Analyze market institutions by demand, not just supply.

Do not ask, "Why are there not more rating agencies?" Ask:

- Who would pay for ratings?
- Who would use them?
- What rule makes them necessary?
- What transaction fails without them?
- What risk is currently hidden by not rating?
- What would change after a default cycle?

The author's style is institutional realism. A market tool does not work just because it exists. It works when incentives require it.

## Page 14 - GDP Is Not Cash Flow

Another recurring method is to challenge macro aggregates that hide funding mechanics. GDP growth can coexist with weak cash flow, rising leverage, or fragile credit quality.

GDP is an output measure. It does not tell you:

- Who financed the activity.
- Whether cash was collected.
- Whether profits were realized.
- Whether growth came from asset inflation.
- Whether credit quality improved.
- Whether the banking system can roll the debt.

AI instruction: When GDP is cited as evidence of strength, ask for the financing mirror.

Financing mirror:

- Which sector borrowed?
- Which sector saved?
- Which sector absorbed the asset?
- Did deposits rise with loans?
- Did external balances improve or deteriorate?
- Did inventory, receivables, or property values carry the growth?
- Did public investment, credit expansion, or exports drive the number?

Author-style framing:

"Strong GDP does not automatically mean strong balance sheets. The macro question is whether the growth generates cash flow sufficient to service the liabilities created to produce it."

This is essential for analyzing credit-driven economies.

## Page 15 - Credit Impulse Beats Static Credit Growth

The author often thinks in changes, not levels. Credit growth itself matters, but the change in credit growth can matter more for asset prices and activity. This is the credit impulse idea.

Credit impulse asks: Is new credit accelerating or decelerating relative to the economy?

Why it matters:

- Asset prices respond to marginal buying power.
- Developers need refinancing, not just historical credit.
- Banks face stress when credit creation slows but debt service remains.
- GDP can lag credit turning points.

AI instruction: Use a three-layer credit view.

1. Stock: total credit outstanding.
2. Flow: new credit extended in the period.
3. Impulse: change in new credit relative to GDP or prior trend.

Then connect impulse to:

- Property transactions.
- Equity market liquidity.
- Corporate bond issuance.
- Bank deposit competition.
- FX pressure from import or capital demand.

Author-style question:

"Is the system receiving enough new credit to validate existing asset prices?"

If the answer is no, the system may look fine in stock terms but weaken at the margin.

## Page 16 - Collateralization Can Become Monetization

The corpus uses the idea that credit in Vietnam is often built on collateral and asset monetization. This is more than standard lending. It is a macro structure where assets become the gateway to liquidity.

Collateralization:

- A borrower pledges an asset.
- The bank lends against it.
- Asset value supports credit.

Monetization:

- Asset appreciation creates more borrowing capacity.
- Borrowed money supports more transactions.
- The asset becomes a liquidity engine.

Risk:

- If asset prices stagnate, refinancing weakens.
- If collateral values fall, credit contracts.
- If banks extend to avoid recognition, zombie behavior grows.
- If policy relies on asset monetization, real reform becomes harder.

AI instruction: For any credit boom, identify the monetized asset.

Questions:

- What asset is being converted into borrowing power?
- Who accepts it as collateral?
- How is it valued?
- What haircut is applied?
- Is repayment based on operating cash flow or refinancing?
- Does the asset price depend on continued credit?

Author-style conclusion:

"The issue is not only leverage. The issue is whether the system has confused asset price appreciation with repayment capacity."

## Page 17 - Zombies Are A Policy Outcome

The author uses "zombie" logic to describe firms or borrowers kept alive by credit support, delayed recognition, or refinancing. The lesson: zombies are not accidents. They often emerge from incentive-compatible delay.

Zombies appear when:

- Banks roll loans to avoid recognizing losses.
- Regulators prefer stability over write-offs.
- Borrowers have collateral but weak cash flow.
- Credit targets encourage extension.
- Asset prices are defended to protect balance sheets.

AI instruction: When analyzing weak borrowers, ask whether the system is resolving losses or carrying them.

Zombie diagnostic:

- Interest coverage is weak.
- Debt is refinanced rather than repaid.
- Collateral value matters more than operating cash flow.
- Banks avoid provisioning.
- Policy creates incentives to delay default.
- New credit funds old obligations.

Macro impact:

- Capital is trapped.
- Productive firms face higher funding costs.
- Banks look stable but lose lending flexibility.
- Asset prices are supported artificially.
- Future policy space shrinks.

Author-style framing:

"The cost of nurturing zombies is not only bad credit. It is the gradual loss of balance-sheet adaptability."

## Page 18 - The Best Macro Analysis Tracks Who Absorbs Losses

Every policy solution has a loss absorber. If an analysis does not identify that actor, it is incomplete.

Potential loss absorbers:

- Central bank: reserves, balance-sheet risk, credibility.
- Treasury: fiscal cost, debt issuance.
- Banks: NIM compression, provisions, capital hits.
- Corporates: refinancing cost, FX loss, margin compression.
- Households: inflation, deposit returns, asset losses.
- Foreign investors: mark-to-market losses, repatriation constraints.
- Future growth: delayed adjustment and lower productivity.

AI instruction: Add a "loss absorber" paragraph to every macro report.

Questions:

- If the exchange rate is defended, who pays?
- If rates are capped, who loses margin or access?
- If property prices fall, whose collateral is impaired?
- If credit is rolled, who carries the bad asset?
- If gold is monetized, who takes price and liquidity risk?
- If fiscal spending rises, who absorbs inflation or debt?

Author-style conclusion:

"The policy may appear successful because the cost has not disappeared; it has been assigned to a balance sheet that has not yet reported it."

This is one of the strongest reasoning habits to copy.

## Page 19 - Global Macro Enters Through Dollar Plumbing

The author connects Vietnam macro to global USD conditions, U.S. Treasury supply, Fed liquidity, reserve status, gold, and tariffs. The connection is rarely abstract. It is usually through dollar funding, reserve assets, capital flows, and expectations.

Global variables to map:

- Fed balance sheet and liquidity operations.
- U.S. Treasury issuance and global reserve demand.
- Dollar index and real rates.
- Foreign investor risk appetite.
- Trade policy and tariffs.
- Gold as trust and liquidity hedge.
- China and regional currency pressure.

AI instruction: Connect global macro to local channels.

Do not say "Fed cuts are good for Vietnam" without specifying:

- Does it reduce USD funding pressure?
- Does it improve carry into VND assets?
- Does it reduce depreciation expectations?
- Does it ease capital outflow?
- Does it lower imported financial conditions?
- Does it increase gold demand through fiscal/inflation fears?

Author-style framing:

"The global variable matters only if it changes a local constraint. Fed policy affects Vietnam through USD liquidity, interest differentials, reserve management, capital flows, and market psychology, not through a simple headline correlation."

## Page 20 - U.S. Treasuries Are The Core Reserve Asset And A Global Liability

The corpus treats U.S. Treasuries not merely as bonds but as the center of global reserve architecture. The U.S. exports safe assets, duration, and dollar claims. That creates both privilege and fragility.

UST analysis should include:

- Supply: fiscal deficits and issuance.
- Demand: foreign reserves, banks, funds, collateral users.
- Yield: inflation, term premium, Fed policy, liquidity premium.
- Currency: USD strength or weakness.
- Global effect: reserve accumulation, dollar liquidity, risk appetite.

AI instruction: When discussing U.S. debt, avoid moral framing. Use plumbing.

Questions:

- Who must buy the new issuance?
- At what yield?
- Does issuance drain liquidity from risk assets?
- Does the Treasury curve create global collateral or crowd out demand?
- Does Fed action offset Treasury supply?
- Does gold respond to fiscal credibility or liquidity expectations?

Author-style point:

"The U.S. can export financial claims because the world uses those claims as reserves. But the same mechanism can transmit inflation, rate pressure, and balance-sheet stress globally."

This is how to connect U.S. fiscal policy to emerging-market FX and liquidity.

## Page 21 - Reserve Currency Status Is Institutional, Not Sentimental

The author is skeptical of simplistic claims that another currency can easily replace the USD. Reserve status is not only about size or ambition. It requires convertibility, trust, asset depth, legal clarity, and willingness to supply safe assets to the world.

Reserve currency checklist:

- Deep government bond market.
- Open capital account or credible convertibility.
- Rule-of-law confidence.
- Liquid FX market.
- Global payment network.
- Crisis-time demand.
- Political willingness to run external deficits or supply assets.
- Low convertibility risk.

AI instruction: Analyze reserve status as infrastructure.

Questions:

- Can foreigners hold large amounts without exit fear?
- Is there enough safe collateral?
- Can the issuer tolerate global use of its currency?
- Is monetary policy transparent enough?
- Are sanctions, controls, or political risk priced in?
- Does the currency strengthen in crises?

Author-style framing:

"A currency does not become a reserve asset because people dislike the current reserve. It becomes a reserve asset because the institutional plumbing allows the world to store and move value at scale."

## Page 22 - Bottom-Up Analysis Misses The Constraint Hierarchy

The author criticizes bottom-up analysis when it treats company drivers as independent of macro constraints. Bottom-up work is useful, but it often fails when funding, FX, regulation, or liquidity dominate company-level fundamentals.

Constraint hierarchy:

1. Legal and regulatory constraint.
2. Funding and liquidity constraint.
3. Currency and external balance constraint.
4. Sector credit constraint.
5. Company cash-flow constraint.
6. Valuation and narrative.

AI instruction: Before building a company view, identify the macro constraint that can override it.

Questions:

- Can the company refinance?
- Can customers access credit?
- Is the sector politically supported or restricted?
- Is the company exposed to FX mismatch?
- Does bank liquidity affect demand?
- Does regulation change the business model?
- Is valuation driven by index weight or real cash flow?

Author-style conclusion:

"Bottom-up analysis can be internally consistent and still wrong if it assumes the macro funding environment remains available."

This does not reject bottom-up analysis. It ranks it below binding constraints.

## Page 23 - Indexes Can Lie Through Concentration

The corpus includes analysis of index bias, especially where a few large names dominate performance. The lesson: index-level conclusions can be misleading when concentration is high.

Index analysis should include:

- Weight concentration.
- Sector concentration.
- Family or conglomerate concentration.
- Liquidity concentration.
- Foreign ownership limits.
- Passive flow effects.
- Difference between equal-weight and cap-weight performance.

AI instruction: When an index moves, decompose it.

Questions:

- Which names contributed most?
- How would the index perform without top constituents?
- Is the move broad or concentrated?
- Are flows passive, foreign, domestic retail, or institutional?
- Does index strength reflect macro strength or one balance-sheet story?

Author-style framing:

"Buying the index may effectively mean buying a concentrated macro bet. If one family or sector dominates the index, the index is not a clean proxy for the economy."

This is especially important in smaller or less diversified markets.

## Page 24 - Administrative Price Controls Create Shadow Prices

Deposit caps, lending guidance, FX bands, and other administrative controls can suppress official prices. But when the official price cannot clear the market, a shadow price emerges.

Shadow prices include:

- Informal FX premium.
- Gold premium.
- Non-price credit rationing.
- Fees or relationship requirements.
- Longer settlement delays.
- Off-balance-sheet structures.
- Implicit guarantees.

AI instruction: When a price is controlled, find the uncontrolled substitute.

Questions:

- Is quantity rationed?
- Is the same exposure priced elsewhere?
- Is there a black-market or parallel-market rate?
- Are participants using gold, USD cash, offshore accounts, or derivatives?
- Is the official price relevant for marginal transactions?
- Does the control stabilize expectations or create pent-up pressure?

Author-style conclusion:

"The official price may be stable because the adjustment moved into quantity, access, or shadow-market pricing."

Never analyze a controlled market without searching for the shadow price.

## Page 25 - Scenario Analysis Should Be Path-Dependent

The author often reasons in scenarios, but not as static bull/base/bear cases. The path matters because balance sheets react differently depending on sequence.

Example:

- If FX pressure rises before deposit rates rise, banks face one constraint.
- If deposit competition rises before credit demand improves, margins compress.
- If property prices fall before banks provision, collateral stress appears.
- If Fed cuts before domestic reform, pressure may ease temporarily but not solve structure.

AI instruction: Build path-dependent scenarios.

Scenario template:

| Step | Event | Immediate balance-sheet effect | Behavioral response | Next constraint |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | USD pressure rises | Reserves/forwards used | Firms hedge, households buy USD/gold | VND rates |
| 2 | Deposit rates rise | Bank funding cost increases | Lending rates lag | NIM compression |
| 3 | Credit slows | Asset transactions weaken | Collateral value questioned | NPL/provisions |

Author-style phrasing:

## Page 31 - Case Method: When Policy Rates Fall But Deposit Rates Stay Sticky

This is one of the most important cases because many macro reports make the same mistake: they assume the official policy rate directly controls the deposit curve. In a clean textbook system, policy rates influence interbank rates, interbank rates influence bank funding costs, and bank funding costs influence lending and deposit rates. In a segmented emerging-market banking system, that chain can break.

Professional case setup:

- Event: central bank cuts or signals lower policy rates.
- Consensus conclusion: funding costs should fall and risk assets should benefit.
- Hidden assumption: official policy rates are the marginal funding price for banks.
- Possible reality: banks may still compete for stable household and corporate deposits.

Balance-sheet mechanism:

Commercial banks fund long-term loans with a mix of CASA, term deposits, interbank funding, wholesale borrowing, equity, and retained earnings. If loan growth is strong, CASA is weak, or depositors demand higher compensation for inflation and FX risk, the bank's true marginal funding cost may be the deposit rate, not the policy rate.

AI diagnostic:

1. Compare policy rate versus interbank rates.
2. Compare interbank rates versus term deposit rates.
3. Compare deposit rates across large state banks, private banks, and smaller banks.
4. Check whether deposit competition is concentrated in weaker or faster-growing banks.
5. Check whether credit growth targets exceed stable funding growth.
6. Check whether FX pressure raises the required VND compensation.

Example interpretation:

"The rate cut helps the short end of the interbank curve, but it does not automatically lower bank funding costs. If banks still need six-to-twelve-month deposits to support loan books, deposit competition remains the binding price. The market should therefore watch term deposit pricing, not the policy headline."

AI output format:

| Layer | Observed move | Interpretation | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Policy rate | Down | Easing signal | May be symbolic |
| Interbank | Down or stable | Reserve liquidity is comfortable | Not enough for credit expansion |
| Deposits | Sticky or rising | Stable funding still scarce | NIM compression |
| Lending | Slow to fall | Borrowers do not get full relief | Credit impulse weakens |
| FX | Under pressure | VND rates need a floor | Rate cuts constrained |

Professional conclusion:

The question is not "Did the central bank ease?" The question is "Which price actually funds the balance sheet that creates credit?"

## Page 32 - Case Method: Abundant Money, Scarce Cash

This case explains the paradox that appears repeatedly in the corpus: the system can have abundant money while participants still feel funding stress. The contradiction disappears once you separate money stock from usable cash flow.

Definitions:

- Money stock: broad monetary aggregates, deposits, claims in the banking system.
- Cash flow: the actual movement of settlement-ready resources to the actor that needs them.
- Usable liquidity: funds in the correct form, tenor, currency, and legal location.

Professional case setup:

- Event: M2 or deposits are high, but rates rise.
- Consensus conclusion: something is irrational or manipulated.
- Hidden assumption: all money is equally usable.
- Better conclusion: liquidity is trapped, mismatched, or unwilling to transform.

Mechanisms that produce the paradox:

- Deposits exist but are locked in long tenors or concentrated in institutions that will not lend.
- Interbank liquidity exists but cannot substitute for sticky retail deposits.
- Corporates have assets but not cash.
- Banks have reserves but lack capital room.
- Households hold gold or cash outside the formal deposit system.
- Credit demand is for long-term funding while available money is short-term.

AI analysis table:

| Question | Weak answer | Professional answer |
|---|---|---|
| Is there money? | Yes, M2 is high | Where is it and who can use it? |
| Are rates high? | That contradicts liquidity | It may reflect scarce term funding |
| Can banks lend? | They have deposits | They also need capital, ratios, and risk appetite |
| Can policy solve it? | Inject reserves | Only if reserves are the binding constraint |

Case conclusion:

If rates rise despite abundant money, do not assume manipulation. Assume the relevant money is not the same as the measured money. Then find the conversion bottleneck.

AI prompt:

"Explain why money aggregates can grow while funding conditions tighten. Separate monetary quantity from liquidity quality, then identify which actor is unable or unwilling to transform claims into cash."

## Page 33 - Case Method: FX Defense With Forward Sales

Forward sales are seductive because they appear to defend the currency without immediately spending spot reserves in the same way. But the author-style analysis asks whether the tool creates a durable flow or only shifts pressure through time.

- Consensus conclusion: FX pressure is controlled.
- Hidden assumption: postponing demand equals solving demand.
- Better question: what happens at settlement?

Forward-sale balance sheet:

- Today: market receives future USD assurance; immediate spot demand may soften.

- Banks/corporates: adjust hedging and payment timing.
- Future date: obligation must be met, rolled, or offset by real inflow.

- Forward points and implied interest differential.

AI scenario:

Base case:

Bad case:

Forward sales delay adjustment, but underlying USD demand remains. Settlement pressure reappears, credibility weakens, and the market demands a higher premium.

Professional conclusion:

Forward intervention is a bridge. A bridge is useful only if there is land on the other side.

AI output:

When analyzing a forward intervention, always include:

- Immediate pressure relieved.
- Future obligation created.
- Flow expected to fill the gap.
- Credibility cost if the flow does not arrive.
- Shadow-market signal to confirm whether pressure truly eased.

## Page 34 - Case Method: Gold Monetization And The Hidden Hedge Problem

Gold monetization looks attractive because households may hold a large stock of idle gold. The superficial policy idea is simple: mobilize gold, convert it into productive funding, and reduce pressure on the currency. The professional analysis is much harder.

The key question:

Who takes the gold price risk, funding risk, FX risk, and liquidity risk?

Possible structure:

1. Household deposits gold.
2. Bank or institution issues a claim against gold.
3. Gold is lent, swapped, sold, or used as collateral.
4. VND or USD funding is created.

| Risk | Description | Natural owner | Problem |
|---|---|---|---|

- Can institutions hedge in global markets?

- What happens if gold rises 20 percent quickly?

Author-style conclusion:

"The problem is not whether gold can be mobilized. The problem is whether the system can absorb the hedge, liquidity, and trust risk created by turning a household store-of-value asset into funding."

Use when the market focuses on the wrong variable.

Pattern 2: The constraint

"The binding constraint is not the amount of money, but its form, tenor, and owner."

Use when liquidity is misunderstood.

Pattern 3: The institutional caveat

## Page 49 - Example Full Note: VND Pressure And Deposit Rates

Hypothetical event:

VND weakens while the central bank signals easier domestic liquidity. Analysts argue that lower rates will support growth and equities.

Professional note:

The common reading is that easier policy should reduce funding costs and support risk assets. That reading is incomplete because it assumes the policy rate is the binding price in the system.

The actual constraint may be the interaction between VND confidence and deposit competition. If households and corporates expect depreciation, banks may need to offer higher VND compensation to keep deposits stable. In that case, the deposit curve can remain sticky even if interbank liquidity improves.

The central bank can ease reserves, but it cannot force depositors to ignore FX risk. If the official FX market is managed, pressure may appear in forward points, gold premium, informal USD pricing, or term deposit competition. These shadow prices are more useful than the policy statement.

For banks, the risk is NIM compression. Deposit costs rise quickly, while loan yields may reprice slowly due to competition, borrower weakness, or policy guidance. That means bank earnings can look fine today while future credit capacity deteriorates.

The next constraint to watch is whether deposit competition spreads from smaller banks to the broader system. If it does, the rate-cut narrative is weak. If deposit rates follow interbank rates lower without FX premium widening, the easing is more credible.

Dashboard:

- USD/VND official and informal premium.
- Forward points.
- Gold domestic premium.
- Interbank rates.
- Six-to-twelve-month deposit rates.
- CASA ratio.
- Bank NIM guidance.

Conclusion:

This is not a simple easing story. It is a test of whether domestic liquidity can be eased without weakening currency confidence and raising the true marginal funding cost.

## Page 50 - Example Full Note: Property Deleveraging Without A Banking Shock

Hypothetical event:

Authorities announce measures to reduce real estate speculation and improve affordability. Analysts expect lower property prices and healthier long-term growth.

Professional note:

The goal may be correct, but the macro path is not automatic. Real estate is not only an asset class. It is collateral infrastructure for the banking system, a funding channel for developers, a store of household wealth, and often a revenue base for local governments.

If property prices fall gradually because supply improves and speculative leverage declines, the system can become healthier. But if prices fall before developer debt, bank collateral, and household expectations are adjusted, the policy can create a credit shock.

The hidden assumption in simple affordability analysis is that lower prices only affect buyers and sellers. In reality, lower prices affect loan-to-value ratios, refinancing capacity, developer liquidity, bank provisioning, and local fiscal flows.

The professional question is not "Should prices fall?" The question is "Who finances the transition from a property-led credit model to a more productive credit model?"

Policy needs a bridge:

- Transparent restructuring of weak developers.
- Bank provisioning capacity.
- Alternative credit channels for productive sectors.
- Supply-side reform rather than only demand suppression.
- Better rental and social housing mechanisms.
- Reduced reliance on land-driven fiscal revenue.

The next constraint to watch is refinancing. If developers cannot roll debt and banks are forced to extend credit only to avoid default, the system is not deleveraging; it is delaying recognition. If new funding supports completion of viable projects while losses are allocated clearly, deleveraging can become constructive.

Dashboard:

- Developer bond maturity schedule.
- Property transaction volume.
- Bank real estate exposure.
- Mortgage growth.
- Land auction activity.
- NPL and restructured loan data.
- Construction activity.
- Household credit demand.

Conclusion:

Real estate reform is macro reform. It succeeds only if the system replaces property collateral dependence with cash-flow-based credit creation. Otherwise, lower prices may simply reveal the losses that rising prices used to hide.

## Page 54 - Banking Book Versus Trading Book

The Bank FS and Return/Limit notes draw a sharp distinction between banking book and trading book. This distinction is essential for macro analysis because it changes P&L recognition, risk limits, capital usage, and management behavior.

Banking book:

- Usually hold-to-maturity.
- Income is mostly carry or spread.
- Main risk is credit risk and IRRBB.
- P&L is mostly realized/accrual.
- Funded by TT1 and sometimes TT2.
- Focus is position size, liquidity ratio, capital, and carry after cost of funds.

Trading book:

- Active market risk position.
- Income includes carry and price movement.
- Main risks are market risk, credit/counterparty risk, stop loss, VaR.
- P&L includes unrealized MtM.
- Funded mostly through TT2 or internal treasury funding.
- Focus is position, DV01, stop loss, VaR, liquidity, and exit price.

AI diagnostic:

Ask:

- Is the desk trying to earn carry or trade price movement?
- Is the position allowed to be marked to market?
- Is the risk limit based on notional, DV01, VaR, stop loss, or credit line?
- Is accounting P&L aligned with economic P&L?
- Can the asset be transferred between books through a technical solution?

Professional warning:

Book classification can shape behavior. If accounting does not show a loss until sale, a desk may delay realizing losses. If MtM triggers stop loss, a desk may want to move the asset into banking book. This does not eliminate the economic risk; it changes recognition and governance.

## Page 55 - Economic Return: Carry Is Not Enough

The attached Bank FS notes define a practical economic return logic:

Economic return for banking assets:

Return - cost of fund - cost of capital for credit risk.

Economic return for trading assets:

Return - cost of fund - cost of capital for credit risk and market risk.

This is powerful because many investors and bankers look only at coupon or accounting yield. A professional treasury view asks whether the asset clears all costs.

- Coupon or contractual yield.
- Accrued interest.
- Price gain or loss.
- Funding cost.
- Capital cost.
- Expected credit loss.
- Market risk cost.
- Liquidity cost.
- Hedge cost.

| Component | Question |
|---|---|
| Yield | What is the contractual return? |

A 7 percent asset funded at 5 percent is not automatically a 2 percent opportunity. After capital, expected loss, liquidity, hedge, and accounting costs, it may be unattractive.

## Page 58 - Fund Transfer Pricing: The Bank's Internal Interest-Rate System

Fund Transfer Pricing (FTP) is the internal price of money. It allocates funding cost between branches, business units, treasury, and ALM.

Why FTP exists:

- Every asset has a funding cost.
- Every liability funds some asset.
- Business lines need a fair cost of funds.
- Management needs to steer growth and profitability.
- Liquidity ratios must be managed across the bank.

FTP objectives:

- Balance profitability between units.
- Align product pricing with funding cost.
- Encourage desired deposits or loans.
- Penalize liquidity-intensive assets.
- Support LDR and short-term funding ratio management.
- Control NIM.

Two broad models:

- Market-based FTP: internal price follows interbank or market curves.
- Product FTP: internal price differs by product, strategy, liquidity value, and business target.

AI instruction:

When analyzing a bank product, include FTP:

Loan yield - FTP cost - expected loss - capital cost - operating cost = true contribution.

Professional example:

A branch books a long-term loan at 9 percent. If FTP for matching maturity is 6.5 percent, expected loss is 1 percent, and capital cost is 1 percent, the economic spread is only 0.5 percent before operating cost. The loan may look profitable in nominal terms but weak on economic contribution.

Professional conclusion:

FTP is the hidden mechanism through which macro funding stress changes bank behavior.

## Page 59 - FTP And NIM: Why Product Pricing Can Mislead

NIM is not just loan yield minus deposit cost at the branch level. FTP determines how income is allocated and which business line appears profitable.

Important FTP effects:

- Raising FTP for long-term funding discourages long-term lending.
- Paying higher FTP for desired deposits encourages branches to raise stable funding.
- Product FTP can subsidize strategic products.
- Market FTP can pass TT2 volatility into business pricing.
- Special FTP can hide or reveal true product economics.

AI diagnostic:

Ask:

- Is the bank using one-price FTP or product-specific FTP?
- Does FTP reflect actual cost of funds or policy target?
- Which unit earns margin: branch, ALM, treasury, or product owner?
- Are liquidity costs embedded in FTP?
- Is capital cost included separately?

Case:

If deposit rates rise but FTP is slow to reprice, lending units may continue booking loans that look profitable. Later, when FTP catches up, NIM compresses. This creates a lag between macro stress and reported earnings.

Professional conclusion:

NIM analysis without FTP is incomplete because the bank's internal price of money determines who feels stress first.

## Page 62 - Repo, Haircuts, And Secured Funding

Repo is a secured borrowing transaction. The notes define it practically: sell an asset today and commit to buy it back later at a higher price. The difference is the repo interest cost.

Example:

- Collateral market value: 100.
- Cash received: 90.
- Repurchase price: 95.
- Haircut: (100 - 90) / 100 = 10 percent.
- Repo rate: (95 - 90) / 90.

Why repo matters:

- Converts bonds into cash.
- Provides secured funding.
- Supports money-market liquidity.
- Links bond market and money market.
- Becomes crucial when clean lines are limited.

AI diagnostic:

- What collateral is eligible?
- What haircut applies?
- Is collateral government bond, guaranteed bond, or corporate bond?
- Is tenor overnight, one week, one month, or longer?
- Is repo rate near OMO ceiling?
- Are haircuts rising?

Professional interpretation:

If repo rates rise and haircuts widen, the market is not only short money; it is questioning collateral liquidity or counterparty capacity. If repo remains available but clean borrowing disappears, the issue is credit line confidence.

Professional conclusion:

Repo is where liquidity, collateral quality, and interest rates meet.

## Page 64 - Reserve Requirement, LCR, LDR, And Short-Term Funding Ratios

## Page 71 - Public Investment And Government Bond Supply

The Factor and KBNN notes highlight the effect of delayed or accelerated public investment.

If public investment is delayed:

- Government cash may accumulate.
- Bond issuance pressure may decline.
- Government bond yields can fall.
- Real-economy stimulus may be weaker.

If public investment accelerates:

- Cash leaves government accounts and enters contractors, suppliers, and workers.
- Liquidity may improve in the real economy.
- Bond issuance may rise if funding is needed.
- Construction and infrastructure sectors may benefit.
- Inflation or import demand may rise depending on project composition.

AI diagnostic:

Ask:

- Is spending funded by existing cash or new bond issuance?
- Are contractors domestic or import-heavy?
- Does spending enter deposits quickly?
- Does it create loan repayment capacity?
- Does it increase inflation in materials or construction?

Professional conclusion:

Public investment can ease liquidity through spending or tighten rates through bond supply. The net effect depends on timing and funding source.

## Page 72 - Fixed Income: Curve Shapes And Macro Meaning

The Fixed Income notes define three core curve shapes:

- Normal upward sloping: short rates lower than long rates.
- Flat: short and long rates similar.
- Inverted: short rates higher than long rates.

Professional interpretation:

Normal curve:

- Market expects higher future rates or requires term premium.
- Carry and roll-down may be attractive if curve remains stable.

Flat curve:

- Term premium compressed.
- Market may expect policy turning point.
- Carry advantage of longer tenor is limited.

Inverted curve:

- Short funding stress or expected future easing.
- Carry trades become dangerous if funded short.
- Banking NIM can compress if liabilities reprice faster than assets.

AI diagnostic:

For any curve:

- Is the move policy-driven or liquidity-driven?
- Did short tenor or long tenor move more?
- Is the curve reflecting inflation, credit, liquidity, or supply?
- Is there a reliable benchmark/fixing?
- Is the bond curve linked to deposit and loan curves?

Professional conclusion:

The curve is not a forecast by itself. It is a price map of funding, duration, inflation, liquidity, and policy credibility.

## Page 73 - Curve Moves: Parallel, Steepening, Flattening, Curvature

The Fixed Income notes separate curve shape from curve movement.

Parallel shift:

- All tenors move similarly.
- Often macro-level repricing.
- Duration dominates P&L.

Flattening:

- Short rates rise more than long rates, or long rates fall more than short rates.
- Can reflect policy tightening, growth slowdown, or near-term funding stress.

Steepening:

- Long rates rise more than short rates, or short rates fall more than long rates.
- Can reflect easing expectations, inflation risk, supply pressure, or term premium.

Curvature:

- Intermediate maturities move differently from front and long end.
- Important for butterfly trades and hedged bond positions.

AI instruction:

When writing a bond view, specify:

- Direction: rates up or down.
- Tenor: front, belly, long end.
- Shape: flattening or steepening.
- Driver: policy, liquidity, supply, inflation, credit, flow.
- Strategy: outright duration, carry/roll-down, curve trade, repo-funded trade.

Professional conclusion:

"Rates will fall" is not a professional bond view. A professional view says which tenor, why, how funded, and what curve move produces P&L.

## Page 74 - Fixed Income Return: Carry Versus Trading P&L

The Fixed Income and MtM notes decompose bond return.

Total realized economics:

Dirty sale price - dirty purchase price - cost of funds + coupons received.

Equivalent decomposition:

- Realized trading: clean sale price - clean purchase price.
- Realized carry: accrued interest change + coupon received - cost of funds.

Key terms:

- Clean price: price excluding accrued interest.
- Dirty price: clean price plus accrued interest.
- Accrued interest: interest earned but not yet paid.
- Cost of funds: borrowing or internal funding cost.

AI diagnostic:

For a bond trade, calculate:

1. Coupon income.
2. Accrued interest change.
3. Clean price change.
4. Funding cost over holding period.
5. Repo or internal FTP cost.
6. Net realized return.
7. Unrealized MtM if still held.

Professional example:

A bond with high coupon can still lose money if yields rise enough. A low-coupon bond can produce strong trading gain if yields fall. Carry and price P&L must be separated because they come from different risks.

Professional conclusion:

Carry is earned with time. Trading P&L is earned from price movement. Funding cost attacks both.

## Page 75 - Spot Rates, Forward Rates, And No-Arbitrage

The Fixed Income notes explain spot and forward rates.

Spot rate:

- The rate for lending/investing from today to a future date.
- For a zero-coupon bond, YTM equals spot rate for that maturity.

Forward rate:

- The implied future rate between two future periods.
- Derived by no-arbitrage between investing long now versus rolling short.

Simple formula:

(1 + S1) x (1 + f(1,1)) = (1 + S2)^2

Continuous approximation:

S2 x T2 = S1 x T1 + f(1,1) x (T2 - T1)

AI instruction:

Use forward rates to identify what the curve is pricing, but do not treat forwards as guaranteed forecasts. The notes distinguish realized forward indifference from pure expectation assumptions.

Professional interpretation:

If the curve is upward sloping, forward rates may be above current spot rates. A carry/roll-down trade works if the curve does not move as implied or if realized rates come below forwards. It fails if rates rise more than carry can absorb.

Professional conclusion:

Forward rates are break-even rates, not prophecies.

## Page 76 - Carry And Roll-Down Strategy

Carry and roll-down, or riding the yield curve, appears in the Fixed Income notes.

Basic idea:

- Buy a longer bond.
- Earn coupon/carry.
- As time passes, the bond rolls to a shorter maturity.
- If the yield curve is upward sloping and unchanged, the bond's yield may fall as it rolls down the curve, creating price gain.

Assumptions:

- Term structure unchanged or rates fall.
- Funding cost remains manageable.
- Bond remains liquid.
- Credit spread does not widen.
- Position can be financed.

Risks:

- Rates rise.
- Curve steepens against the position.
- Funding cost increases.
- Repo haircut changes.
- Credit spread widens.
- Accounting P&L does not match economic risk.

AI diagnostic:

Before recommending carry/roll-down, calculate:

- Expected carry.
- Expected roll-down.
- Funding cost.
- DV01.
- Break-even rate move.
- Stop-loss level.
- Liquidity and repo availability.

Professional conclusion:

Carry/roll-down is not free yield. It is a leveraged bet that the curve does not move against you faster than time pays you.

## Page 77 - Credit Spread In A Market Without A Clean Benchmark

The Current Issue notes argue that Vietnam lacks a clean credit spread framework because curves are not fully linked and fixing is weak.

Textbook credit spread:

Corporate bond yield - risk-free government bond yield for the same maturity.

Practical problems:

- Corporate bonds may reference TT1 deposit rates.
- Government bonds trade in TT2-style institutional markets.
- There may be no reliable swap curve.
- There may be limited credit rating usefulness.
- Liquidity and relationship pricing distort yield.
- The maturity may not be truly comparable.

AI instruction:

When someone quotes a credit spread, ask:

- What is the benchmark?
- Is it TT1 or TT2?
- Is the corporate bond liquid?
- Is the government curve liquid at the same tenor?
- Is there a swap/fixing curve?
- Is the spread credit, liquidity, tax, embedded option, or relationship premium?

Professional conclusion:

In incomplete markets, "spread" may be a rough label, not a clean risk price.

## Page 78 - MtM Bond Versus Accounting Bond

The MtM Bond vs Accounting notes show why reported P&L can differ from treasury economics.

MtM view:

- Mark to market.
- Include market price changes.
- Include unrealized gains/losses.
- Include funding cost.
- Better for trading risk.

Accounting/accrual view:

- Often carries at cost or amortized value until sale.
- Recognizes coupon and amortization.
- May not include funding cost in the same way.
- Realized P&L appears when sold.

Why this matters:

Total economics from purchase to sale may converge, but timing differs. A desk can delay realization, harvest gains, or move assets between books to manage KPI, stop loss, or limits.

AI diagnostic:

Ask:

- Is the position valued at market?
- Is cost of funds included?
- Is P&L realized or unrealized?
- Is the desk measured on accounting P&L or economic P&L?
- Could KPI incentives force a sale at the wrong time?

Professional conclusion:

Accounting can change when pain is recognized. It does not change whether the pain exists.

## Page 79 - FX Accounting Versus FX MtM

The FX Accounting vs FX MtM notes are crucial for understanding swap, forward, and treasury P&L.

Spot:

- FX position appears immediately.
- MtM should include cost of funds.
- Accounting and treasury may look similar at initiation but diverge in carry treatment.

Forward:

- Accounting may allocate swap points over time and revalue based on spot logic.
- MtM values the remaining forward against current market forward.
- At settlement, both converge economically.

FX swap:

- Economically a near-leg spot plus far-leg forward.
- Often no net FX open position if structured properly.
- Accounting may show positive swap-point accrual.
- MtM may show gain/loss if market swap points change.

AI diagnostic:

For FX products, separate:

- Spot exposure.
- Forward exposure.
- Swap point accrual.
- Cost of VND funds.
- Return on USD funds.
- Accounting P&L.
- MtM P&L.
- KPI treatment.

Professional conclusion:

FX swap P&L can look smooth in accounting and volatile in MtM. The economic question is whether the swap point compensates for funding and market movement.

## Page 80 - Hedging: Risk Is Transferred, Not Destroyed

The Fundamental Hedging notes state the most important principle: fair hedging transfers risk; it does not make risk disappear.

Examples:

- FX swap transfers spot FX risk into interest-rate differential and funding risk.
- Cross-currency swap transfers currency cash-flow risk into counterparty, basis, and rate risk.
- Bond hedging transfers duration risk into funding, basis, and hedge-roll risk.
- Forward hedging locks a price but introduces liquidity and opportunity cost.

AI instruction:

For every hedge, identify:

1. Original risk.
2. Hedge instrument.
3. Risk reduced.
4. Risk created.
5. Basis risk.
6. Counterparty risk.
7. Liquidity/margin risk.
8. Accounting mismatch.

Professional example:

A corporate hedges USD payable with a forward. It removes spot USD/VND uncertainty but creates mark-to-market exposure, counterparty line usage, and potential liquidity need if the hedge must be rolled or closed.

Professional conclusion:

Hedging is balance-sheet surgery. It changes the shape of risk; it does not abolish risk.

## Page 81 - Interest-Rate Parity And FX Swap Points

The hedging notes show interest-rate parity through a simple USD/VND example.

Basic idea:

Forward price reflects spot price plus the interest-rate differential between currencies.

Approximation:

Forward = spot + spot x (VND rate - USD rate) x time.

Swap point:

Forward - spot.

Gap swap:

VND rate - USD rate.

Interpretation:

- If VND rates are higher than USD rates, forward USD/VND usually trades above spot.
- The swap point compensates for funding differential.
- A forward is not a free FX forecast; it embeds interest-rate economics.

AI diagnostic:

Ask:

- What is spot?
- What is forward?
- What is implied VND rate?
- What is implied USD rate?
- Is the swap point rich or cheap relative to funding markets?
- Is there arbitrage after transaction costs and limits?

Professional conclusion:

FX forwards are money-market instruments in disguise.

## Page 82 - Realized And Unrealized P&L

The Return and Limit notes distinguish realized and unrealized profit.

Realized P&L:

- Coupon received.
- Carry accrued and allocated.
- Swap point realized over contract.
- Back-to-back deal margin.
- Closed trading gains/losses.

Unrealized P&L:

- Market price change not yet closed.
- Swap trading revaluation.
- Bond MtM movement.
- Open FX or rate position revaluation.

Why this matters:

Banking book may show mostly realized/accrual P&L. Trading book shows both realized and unrealized. Management incentives and limits differ.

AI diagnostic:

When a desk reports profit, ask:

- Is it realized cash or mark-to-market?
- Is it carry or price movement?
- Is funding cost deducted?
- Is the position still open?
- Is the P&L reversible?
- Is it supported by a hedge?

Professional conclusion:

Unrealized P&L is information, not cash. Realized P&L is cash or closed economics, but even realized gains can be created by selling good assets and leaving bad risks behind.

## Page 83 - Technical Solutions: Book Moves, KPI, And Hidden Incentives

## Page 90 - Final Integrated Framework: Macro To Treasury To Trade

The complete professional workflow now has three layers.

Layer 2: Bank treasury plumbing.

- Does it change FTP?
- Does it change LDR, LCR, short-term funding ratio, capital, or IRRBB?

Layer 3: Market expression.

- FX spot or forward.
- Gold premium.
- Deposit rates.
- Interbank curve.
- Government bond curve.
- Corporate bond spread proxy.
- Bank NIM.
- Equity index breadth.
- Real estate refinancing.

Master AI prompt:

"Analyze this event through three layers. First identify the macro constraint and loss absorber.
